BERKSHIRE HATHAWAY INC.

To the Shareholders of Berkshire Hathaway Inc.:

It won't be long before Greg Abel replaces me as CEO and will be writing these letters. Greg understands that a "report" is what a Berkshire CEO annually owes to owners. He is ready.

Berkshire had a good 2024. Operating earnings reached $47.4 billion, a record for your company. Our results were driven by improved performance across several business segments, with insurance operations—including GEICO and Gen Re—contributing significantly.

We must acknowledge that last year I told you that Berkshire's earnings and intrinsic value would only modestly advance in 2024. This turned out to be very wrong. Our insurance businesses did far better than expected, and investment income from our cash holdings increased substantially as interest rates remained elevated.

Our Businesses

We own a small percentage of a dozen or so very large and highly profitable businesses, household names including Apple. We also own 100% of many businesses, ranging from See's Candies to BNSF. All are managed by CEOs who understand that Berkshire is their permanent home.

Charlie used to emphasize that "problems require action, however uncomfortable that may be." In 2024, we took action where needed. Mistakes in capital allocation and personnel decisions will occur. What matters is recognizing them quickly and correcting course.

A word about hiring: I never look at where a candidate has gone to school. Never! Innate qualities—integrity, intelligence, energy, and the ability to communicate—matter far more than educational pedigree.

Cash and Investments

Berkshire's cash and Treasury bill holdings reached $334.2 billion by year-end, more than double the $163.7 billion we held at the end of 2023. Some will question why we hold so much.

My answer: We would love to spend it on wonderful businesses at sensible prices. When that is not possible, we will hold cash. Paper money can see its value evaporate if fiscal folly prevails. In contrast, productive businesses will usually find a way to cope with monetary instability.

We prefer equities over cash and bonds. Always have, always will. But we will not stretch for returns or compromise our standards.

Japan

Our investments in five major Japanese trading companies have worked out well. We simply looked at their financial records and were amazed at the low prices of their stocks relative to their earnings, dividends, and book values.

These companies practice disciplined capital allocation. They respect shareholders. We increased our positions in 2024 and intend to hold them indefinitely.

Insurance

Property-casualty insurance remains Berkshire's core business. The industry follows a financial model that is rare—very rare—among giant businesses: we collect premiums before paying claims, often holding the money for years.

This "float" has grown to enormous size at Berkshire, and when combined with underwriting profits, it provides us with costless capital for investment. GEICO and our reinsurance operations all performed admirably.

Taxes

In 2024, Berkshire paid $26.8 billion in federal income taxes. To put this in perspective: if Berkshire had sent the Treasury a $1 million check every 20 minutes throughout all of 2024—day and night, 365 days—we still would have owed a significant sum.

We are happy to pay our fair share. America has been very good to Berkshire.

Looking Forward

Berkshire is built to endure. Our collection of businesses, our financial strength, and our culture will outlast any individual, including me.

Greg Abel is ready. The transition will be seamless. I remain as Chairman, and I will be in the office as long as I am useful.


Warren E. Buffett
Chairman of the Board

February 22, 2025
