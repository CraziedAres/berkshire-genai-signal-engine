BERKSHIRE HATHAWAY INC.

To the Shareholders of Berkshire Hathaway Inc.:

Charlie Munger died on November 28, just 33 days before his 100th birthday. Though born and raised in Omaha, he spent 80% of his life domiciled elsewhere. Consequently, it was not until 1959 when he was 35 that I first met him. In 1962, he decided that he should take up money management.

Three years later he told me – correctly! – that I had made a dumb decision in buying control of Berkshire. But, he assured me, since I had already made the move, he would tell me how to correct my mistake. What he said was: "Forget what you know about buying fair businesses at wonderful prices; instead, buy wonderful businesses at fair prices."

In effect, Charlie saw everything about business matters better and faster than I did. His brain was never on autopilot. His insights could not be replicated by anyone else. And Charlie's wisdom was always delivered with a clarity that few of us can match.

The construction of Berkshire as it now stands was largely Charlie's architecture. My primary role has simply been that of general contractor, with Berkshire's subsidiary CEOs acting as subcontractors carrying out Charlie's design.

Let me turn now to our operating results.

Operating Earnings and Investment Results

Our 2023 operating earnings totaled $37.4 billion, compared to $30.8 billion in 2022. All major segments of Berkshire contributed to this improvement.

What I referred to as a casino-like atmosphere on Wall Street remains very much in evidence. Though the market has corrected from its most frenzied valuations, we continue to find very little that excites us in terms of potential acquisitions or stock purchases. The prices simply don't make sense for the returns we require.

We have therefore continued to accumulate cash and Treasury bills. Our holdings of these "equivalents" reached $167.6 billion at year-end, a record for Berkshire. We would much prefer to deploy this capital productively. But we will not do so unless we find opportunities that offer reasonable risk-adjusted returns.

This is not a time to be aggressive. It is a time to be patient.

Insurance Operations

Our insurance operations delivered outstanding results in 2023. Our float – money that doesn't belong to us but that we temporarily hold – reached a record $168.9 billion at year-end, up from $164.1 billion in 2022.

GEICO is back. After struggling for several years, Tony Nicely's successor, Todd Combs, has accomplished a major turnaround. Underwriting discipline has been restored. Policyholder growth has returned. And the company is once again focused on what matters: serving customers well while maintaining pricing discipline.

Our reinsurance businesses also had an excellent year. Ajit Jain continues to demonstrate the wisdom and discipline that have made Berkshire a force in global reinsurance markets. We will not write business at inadequate prices, period. When the market softens, we will simply step away and wait.

I cannot stress enough how valuable our insurance float is to Berkshire. We pay nothing for this $169 billion – indeed, we have been paid to hold it over time through our underwriting profits. It provides the capital base for much of our investment activity.

Railroad and Utilities

BNSF had a challenging year. Volume declined as the overall freight market softened. Competition from trucking intensified. And labor costs continued to rise. Nevertheless, BNSF remains one of the two most essential rail networks in North America, and its competitive position is unassailable.

Our energy operations at Berkshire Hathaway Energy faced headwinds from wildfire-related litigation and regulatory uncertainties. The utility business in America is undergoing significant changes, and we must be prepared for an evolving environment. We remain committed to investing in the infrastructure our country needs, but we will do so with clear eyes about the risks.

The Importance of Holding Wonderful Businesses

Berkshire owns a collection of operating businesses that, taken together, could not be replicated. Our subsidiaries employ nearly 400,000 people. They serve millions of customers. And they are managed by CEOs who love their businesses and treat them as their own.

When we buy a business, we intend to hold it forever. This is not a slogan – it is our modus operandi. When we purchase a stock, we think like owners, not traders. Our favorite holding period is forever.

This long-term orientation gives Berkshire significant advantages. Our managers can make decisions with a 10-year or 20-year horizon. They don't have to worry about quarterly earnings pressures. They can invest in their businesses, their people, and their communities.

The economy will have its ups and downs. Markets will fluctuate. But the great businesses we own will continue to compound their value over time.

Looking Forward

I am often asked about succession at Berkshire. Greg Abel is ready to become CEO when I am no longer in the role. He has the experience, the values, and the intelligence needed to lead this remarkable company. The transition will be seamless.

As for our investment operations, Todd Combs and Ted Weschler continue to manage substantial portfolios with skill and discipline. Their roles will expand over time.

Berkshire is in excellent shape. Our businesses are strong. Our balance sheet is fortress-like. Our culture is embedded. And our managers are first-rate.

I have never been more confident in Berkshire's future. We are built to endure.

Annual Meeting

Berkshire's annual meeting will be held on Saturday, May 4, 2024, in Omaha. Charlie will not be with us in person, but his wisdom will echo through everything we discuss.

Come to Omaha. We will have a good time.


Warren E. Buffett
Chairman of the Board

February 24, 2024
